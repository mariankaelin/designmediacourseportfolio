Integrating Writing and Design Portfolio

Design Beginnings


Design Fuego

The Design Fuego 'I am...' project was my first introduction to HTML and CSS. This website-creation process, based on Pitt's Design Fuego template, provided me with the experience and tools that I'm using to put together this Portfolio. Using this page, I presented a casual and personable introduction to my class, and discussed what I was hoping to bring to, and gain from, this course.

Logo A/B/C

The Logo A/B/C Project introduced me to logo-generation via Canva and Adobe InDesign. My objective was to produce three options for a personal logo by tweaking AI-generated or template designs. I interpreted this assignment as presenting myself in business-form, so my designs take on a company persona. By experimenting with these platforms, I discovered what easy online tools like these can offer, but also what they lack compared to original work.

UX Design Presentation

After reading about proper UX Design in Joel Marsh's book UX For Beginners: A Crash Course in 100 Short Lessons, I conducted a study into the effectiveness of each logo I created. I interviewed three participants with relevant backgrounds, and asked them scripted questions about how each design made them think and feel. I applied Norman's Theory of the Three Levels of Processing (2013) to write questions inquiring about the visceral, behavioral, and reflective reactions of my interviewees.

Cartoon Portrait

This project involved taking a photo of myself, and cartoon-ifying it using Adobe software. I used the pen tool in Adobe Illustrator to draw multiple layers of color blocking work. I used black outlines to make each feature pop, and edited each anchor point to ensure each layer ended up flush with one another.

Good Design

My Good Design graphic identified key details of a magazine cover that showcase why it "works" in terms of viusal appeal. I used Adobe InDesign to create text boxes, choose fun fonts, and narrow in on particular features of this piece. I used major design principles in The Non-Deisgner's Design Book by Robin Williams to analyze this piece, including Contrast, Alignment, and Balance.

Course Goals


1. Consider the role of rhetoric, technology, and ethics in acts of making.

Whenever creating new designs or projects, I believe that it is important to keep inclusivity in mind, to make sure that your work is easily accessible to all that view it. This includes adding descriptions to images when appropriate, and using clear rhetoric where terms are defined, and concepts are easily understood. Additionally, through technology like Adobe software, AI generation tools are at the disposal of digital creators. To ethically produce content, all sources and methods of production must be properly cited and acknowledged, including AI usage.

2. Leverage Design Principles.

As a new designer, I've really benefitted from the course reading The Non-Deisgner's Design Book by Robin Williams, which employs four key design principles: Proximity, Alignment, Repetition, and Contrast. These principles are exapanded on to identify helpful technical concepts for color, typography, balance, rhetoric, and more. In my Good Design piece, my Design Fuego I Am... website, and even my UX Deign Study Presentation that I shared with my classmates, I utilized the book's tips to create appealing work.

3. Engage with others in a workshop environment.

Collaboration is always what helps me most when learning something new. In class, I've volunteered for my work to be shown and commented on, allowing for contructive criticism to guide my revision process. Opening one's work up to discussion, especially from peers that you may not be close to or entirely comfortable with, introduces a real-life component to a class project. Working as a designer in the 'real world' means that your work is always subject to public interpretation, so by collaborating and critiquing respectfully in a learning space, we are strengthening ourselves for future growth.



4. Practice revision within and across media forms.

I edited both my Design Fuego I am... wesbite and my Good Design graphic to insert them in this Portfolio. Consequently, both of these pieces were presented in front of the entire class, and given respectful constructive criticism by my peers and Professor Quigley. These comments and more personal reflection on the purpose of each piece allowed me to fix mistakes and improve them significantly. Revision is a very dear process to me, because it means that everything can always be better than before - we are always working towards a more positive end!

5. Learn to present your work.

As someone who already has years of public speaking experience and is comfortable in front of large groups, I can absolutely conclude that it gets easier each time, but not because the intimidation goes away - instead, because the confidence overrides it. I enjoy this course's emphasis on presentation, because it again drills down that 'real-world' career-focused feel to our work. In a board or meeting room, we need to be comfortable taking ownership of our work through speech rehersal, background research, preperation for follow-up questions, and taking responsibility for necessary improvements. In this course I hope to gain even more confidence in my ability to present information in an accessible, direct, and personable way.
